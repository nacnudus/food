Written byBiscuitcase
Posted12 December 2016
Overall rating 
5 out of 5
Very very tasty...
It's hard to explain the appeal of pickled herring to people who haven't loved them for years - it's a hard sell, really, I think you have to be raised with them. But when I'm trying to coax newcomers to dip a toe in the world of rollmops, I always ease them in gently by recommending these dill marinated herring which are mild, sweet but not overpoweringly so, and still really savoury and satisfying and gutsy in that way that only herring can be. Just to clarify for anyone confused reading the conflicting reviews (I know I was) - these differ markedly to the rollmops from the same producer. The rollmops are a standard whole herring fillet with its skin totally on, rolled up with onions in a faintly sweet but mostly sour vinegary marinade. They're lovely for when you get that craving for cheekbone-squeakingly sour juicy herring. These dill herring differ in that the whole herring fillet is split into its two sides and each side is semi-skinned (there's just a faint thin silveriness left on) and the side is chopped roughly in half. The pieces of individual fillet are about 3 inches long I'd estimate. They're milder and I won't say softer - they absorb more marinade as they're semi-skinned, so they don't need to be sawed through with a knife like a rollmop, they're very easy to cut and eat. I won't say I prefer them to the traditional rollmops - they're two vastly different products which are both delicious depending on which type of herring you're craving at the time, you know? Personally I do love the wholeness of the rollmop and the added bit of texture and firmness it has with its skin, but I also love the milder sweet gentleness of this more refined version. As I said, its a friendlier introduction for the uninitiated and for kids. I probably end up eating more plain rollmops, but I still like to have a tub or two of these chopped fillets in the fridge.
If you're wondering which is better value, well, if you look at the herring content as listed for these dill fillets, it's apparently over 50%, whereas the listing for the rollmops states 38% I think, the rest being marinade and onions and that one peculiar, lonesome slice of gherkin that appears in every tub. I suppose technically you're getting slightly more of the actual herring given its mostly skinless here, but I can't say I've noticed a difference in the weight with the rollmops. I actually weighed the drained contents, and you get 3 rollmops rolled with a few onions, and once you've squeezed off most of the marinade, they weigh around 200g. I drained the dill fillets and squeezed them similarly and there's slightly less onion - the drained contents weighed 190g. So, not a lot in it. Personally I feel like the rollmops are a more substantial product - the 3 whole fillets are usually very plump and generously sized.
Just one tip for herring and onion lovers - they don't give you much onion, sadly, but you can't whine about that given the amount of herring... what I do, though, is very thinly slice two halves of a small red onion into semi circles and then once I've taken the fillets or rollmops out of the tub, I keep the marinade in there and just put my onions in it, stir them around to separate the slices, and leave them to steep for a little while. They absorb all the nice flavour of the marinade and they're lovely to serve with the fish. If you're like me and you can't make up your mind which variety you fancy, then why not have a rollmop fillet with half a tub of the dill fillet pieces. The contrast is pretty fun in a tangy-sweet ying and yang kinda way. And if your nearest and dearest find the rollmops too sour, you can always do the onion trick with them and steep them for a while in the dill marinade. Either way, in any way, they're both extremely tasty and apparently good for you too! Perfect excuse to wheel out the sticky toffee pud and clotted cream for afters... ;)
Yes, I recommend this product.
